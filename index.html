LOD Summary

Our LOD framework follows the AIA / BIMForum LOD Specification, adapted for as-built modeling. Because our models are built from field-documented existing conditions rather than design intent, we apply these standards to reflect what is visible and verifiable.

Our standard deliverable is LOD 250 — a PPM-defined tier, not a formally defined AIA level. It sits between LOD 200 and LOD 300: some elements align more closely with one, some with the other, and many fall in between. Classifying our work at LOD 250 gives a clear benchmark that balances accuracy with efficiency.

LOD vs. LOA

Level of Development (LOD) and Level of Accuracy (LOA) describe two different aspects of a model.

LOD defines the level of geometric development and detail included in modeled elements. LOA defines how closely the modeled geometry represents measured field conditions. These are established independently, meaning a highly detailed element is not necessarily more accurate.

Unless otherwise noted in the project scope, PPM Scan-to-BIM deliverables are produced to a target represented accuracy of ±1 inch relative to visible, accessible, and adequately field-documented existing conditions. This target is generally aligned with USIBD LOA 20.

LOD Comparison

images/lod-200.png

LOD 200

Generic

A simplified 3D model with verified overall geometry based on field-documented conditions, including size, location, and orientation. Elements are modeled as generic objects with minimal or omitted materials and detailing.

images/lod-250.png

LOD 250

PPM Standard

PPM Standard

Our standard as-built BIM deliverable. It includes verified overall geometry with a consistent mix of LOD 200 generic elements and LOD 300 specific elements, keeping the model efficient while still highly usable.

images/lod-300.png

LOD 300

Specific

A higher-detail BIM model where elements are modeled as specific components. Elements include accurate size, shape, and placement, with added detail such as materials, profiles, and major architectural features when they are visible and verifiable. *This does not include fabrication-level detail or concealed construction, such as finished wall, floor, ceiling, or roof assemblies.*
